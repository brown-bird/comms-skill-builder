Playbook › Negotiating a flexible working arrangement

Situation

You want to negotiate a change to your working arrangement — more remote days, adjusted hours, or a hybrid schedule. Your manager has legitimate concerns about team consistency or visibility. Both of you have real interests here. This isn't just a request; it's a negotiation, and how you frame it determines whether it becomes a productive conversation or a stalemate.

Applicable Principles

Principled Negotiation Clarity & Brevity

What to Avoid

  • Framing it as a demand or take-it-or-leave-it — it puts them on the defensive before the conversation starts
  • Relying purely on personal preference: "I just prefer working from home" — connect it to work outcomes instead
  • Vague asks: "more flexibility" is harder to agree to than a specific arrangement
  • Accepting or refusing terms in the moment without thinking through the full implications
  • Ignoring their concerns — address them directly rather than hoping they won't come up

Recommended Approach

  • Know your interests and theirs before the conversation — what do you actually need, and what are their likely concerns about team visibility or consistency?
  • Make a specific, concrete proposal — not "more flexibility" but a defined arrangement with days and conditions
  • Frame it around shared interests: productivity, output quality, and mutual trust
  • Anticipate their concerns and address them proactively: "I know team presence on core days matters — here's how I'd handle that"
  • Propose a trial period if they're hesitant — it significantly lowers the stakes of a yes
  • Know your BATNA going in: what will you do if the answer is no?

Example Language

"I'd like to propose working from home on Mondays and Fridays. My output hasn't changed on remote days — if anything, I do my focused work better at home. I know being present on core team days matters, so I'd commit to being in Tuesday through Thursday. Would you be open to trying it for a month and reviewing from there?"

Follow-Up

If approved, demonstrate the arrangement is working — meet your deadlines, stay responsive, and be visible in team communications. Come back after the trial period proactively to review it rather than waiting to be asked. Managers remember who holds up their end of a flexible arrangement, and who quietly lets the conditions drift.
