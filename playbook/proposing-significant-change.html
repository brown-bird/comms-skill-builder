Playbook › Proposing a significant change

Situation

You want to propose a meaningful change — to a process, structure, team workflow, or strategic direction. The stakes are high enough that a poorly received pitch could sink the idea before it gets a fair hearing.

Principles

  • Before presenting your solution, check that others share your read of the problem — "Does this feel like a real issue to you, or am I overweighting it?"
  • Surface the topic briefly first and gauge the reaction before going deep — a low-stakes early conversation is easier to recover from than a failed formal pitch
  • Frame the problem in terms of its impact on others, not your own frustration or preference
  • Share your proposal as a starting point, not a finished plan — "Here's how I've been thinking about it, but I want to hear what you'd change"
  • After presenting, check for genuine alignment before treating it as agreed — a positive reaction isn't the same as a committed yes

Anti-patterns

  • Presenting the solution before confirming anyone else sees the problem — even a good solution to a problem no one owns will land flat
  • Framing the change as obviously necessary — if others don't share that read, it positions you as dismissive of their perspective before they've had a chance to engage
  • Treating a positive reaction as full commitment — warmth and genuine agreement are different things, and conflating them leads to plans that unravel later

Example Language

"I've been noticing [problem] and I think it's creating real friction. Does that match what you're seeing, or am I missing context?"

"I have some thoughts on how we might address it — but before I go into them, what's your sense of why it keeps happening?"

Follow-Up

After any initial conversation, document what was agreed and what's still open. If the response was lukewarm, don't push for a decision — note the open questions and return with more information. Change proposals often need more than one conversation; what you're building in the first one is shared ownership of the problem, not commitment to your solution.
