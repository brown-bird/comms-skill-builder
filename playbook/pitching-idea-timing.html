Playbook › Pitching an idea when the timing feels off

Situation

You have a proposal you're excited about — a new process, a project, a change in direction. But the timing feels wrong: your manager is stressed, the team just came off a difficult sprint, or there's organizational uncertainty in the air. Push ahead anyway and you risk a flat reception that kills a good idea. Wait indefinitely and the moment may never arrive.

Example Language

Testing the water: "I've been thinking about [problem area] — I'm not sure if now is the right time to dig into it, but I wanted to flag it's on my mind. When would be a good moment to talk through some ideas?"

Naming bad timing directly: "I have something I'd like to bring to you, but I want to wait until the current crunch passes. Can we find 20 minutes next week?"

Principles

  • Read the room: is this a temporary stress moment, or a sustained state? Most temporary crunches pass within days — it's worth waiting
  • Test the water before pitching: surface the problem you're trying to solve, not the solution yet, and gauge the reaction
  • Pick a low-stakes moment — a 1:1, a quiet end of a meeting — rather than a formal presentation slot
  • If the timing is clearly bad, name it rather than forcing it: reserve time and come prepared when conditions are better
  • When you do pitch, invite input early rather than presenting a finished plan — people support what they helped shape

Anti-patterns

  • Forcing the pitch during organizational stress — ideas presented at the wrong moment get anchored to that context and are harder to revive
  • Waiting for a "perfect" moment that never arrives — indefinite delay is its own failure mode
  • Framing it as urgent when it isn't — artificial pressure breeds resistance
  • Presenting a fully formed proposal when early input would build more ownership and support

Follow-Up

After any early conversation, follow up with a brief written note so the idea doesn't evaporate. Even a lukewarm response is more useful than silence — it tells you what you'll need to address. A written record also lets you revisit the idea later without starting from scratch.
