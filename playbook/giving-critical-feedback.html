Playbook › Giving critical feedback to a peer

Situation

You need to share feedback with a peer about something that's affecting their work, your working relationship, or the team. Unlike feedback from a manager, peer feedback isn't formally invited — which means the first challenge isn't the message itself but creating conditions where it can actually be heard.

Recommended Approach

  • Ask before you tell — check whether they're open to feedback before delivering it: "I've noticed something I think might be useful to share — are you open to hearing it?"
  • Describe the specific behavior you observed, not your interpretation of what it means: "In the last two meetings you cut across [name] mid-sentence" not "you're dismissive"
  • Check your read before drawing conclusions: "I don't know if you're aware of it — what's your take?"
  • Share the impact from your own position, not as a general verdict: "From where I sit, it creates tension in the room" not "people find it difficult"
  • Close by naming the relational intent, not by minimizing: "I'm raising this because I value working well with you, not to create a problem between us"

What to Avoid

  • Delivering feedback without checking first — skipping the consent step removes the other person's ability to orient themselves to what's coming, and puts the conversation in adversarial territory from the start
  • Delivering feedback while still carrying frustration about it — what starts as feedback becomes venting, which signals to the other person that the conversation is about you, not them
  • Offering a verdict on character or intent rather than naming a specific behavior: "you don't care about the team"
  • Speaking for others you haven't actually spoken for: "everyone finds it difficult" — it undermines your credibility and puts them on the defensive

Example Language

"Can I share something I've been sitting with? I want to flag it as a peer, not as a verdict — it's yours to take or leave."

"I noticed [specific behavior] in [specific context]. I'm not sure if it landed the way you intended — what was going on for you there?"

Follow-Up

You've done your part once you've shared it clearly and they've had a chance to respond. Don't follow up to check whether they've changed — that tips into management. If the behavior continues and affects you or the team, that's a separate conversation about impact, or a signal it needs to involve a manager.
