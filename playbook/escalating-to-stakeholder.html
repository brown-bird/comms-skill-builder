Playbook › Escalating a problem to a senior stakeholder

Situation

Something has gone wrong — a project is off track, a decision is blocked, a risk is materializing — and you need a senior stakeholder to understand and act. They have limited context and limited time. A poorly structured message will either be ignored or create more confusion than it resolves.

Example Language

"[Project X] is at risk of missing the [date] deadline due to [specific blocker]. I need a decision on [specific ask] by [date] to keep it on track. I'd recommend Option A — [one sentence why] — but wanted your input before moving forward."

For a shorter message: "[Issue] is blocked on [decision/action]. Can you [specific ask] by [date]?"

Principles

  • Lead with the situation in one sentence: what's happening and why it matters now
  • State your ask explicitly — what do you need from them, and by when
  • Provide the minimum context needed to understand the ask — no more
  • If there are options, present them briefly with your recommendation clearly marked
  • Make it easy to respond: a single decision point is better than an open-ended question

Anti-patterns

  • Starting with background context before stating the problem — they may stop reading before reaching your ask
  • Being vague about what you need: "just wanted to flag this" is not an actionable message
  • Overwhelming with detail in an attempt to demonstrate thoroughness — it obscures the signal
  • Burying your recommendation at the end after a long explanation
  • Escalating without having a proposed path forward — come with a recommendation, not just a problem

Follow-Up

Once resolved, close the loop briefly — let them know the outcome of the action they took. Senior stakeholders notice who keeps them informed versus who disappears after getting what they needed. A two-line follow-up builds more trust than most longer interactions.
