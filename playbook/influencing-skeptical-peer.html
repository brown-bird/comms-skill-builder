Playbook › Getting a skeptical peer to support a process change

Situation

You want to introduce a process change that benefits the broader organization — but it creates extra work for a peer on another team. You have no authority over them. They're skeptical, possibly resistant. Getting their genuine buy-in is essential; going around them will damage the relationship and likely undermine the change anyway.

Example Language

"I know this adds work on your side, and I don't want to pretend otherwise. I wanted to come to you before this goes any further because your team's experience will determine whether it actually works. Can you help me understand what your biggest concerns are? I'd rather solve for those now than hand you something finished."

Principles

  • Start with genuine curiosity: understand their concerns before making your case
  • Acknowledge the cost to their team explicitly — don't minimize it
  • Reframe around shared interests: what problem does this solve that affects both of you?
  • Invite them into the design: come with a draft, not a final plan, and mean it
  • Identify what would make a yes easier — reduced scope, phased rollout, support during transition
  • If they remain skeptical, find the smallest version of the change they'd be willing to pilot

Anti-patterns

  • Presenting the change as a done deal that just needs their cooperation — it breeds resentment
  • Leading with organizational benefit while glossing over the real cost to their team
  • Escalating to their manager before genuinely attempting peer-level influence
  • Treating their skepticism as an obstacle to overcome rather than information worth understanding
  • Calling in favors before you've built enough trust for the ask

Applicable Principles

Influencing Without Authority Empathy & Tone Principled Negotiation

Follow-Up

Keep them informed as the change moves forward. Credit their input publicly when presenting the final approach. If the change creates friction for their team once it's live, address it quickly and visibly — it signals that their concerns were taken seriously, not just collected. This is the kind of follow-through that makes future influence possible.
