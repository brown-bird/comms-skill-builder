Principle 10

Regulate before you respond

Self-Regulation

What it is

Before you can communicate effectively, you need to manage your own internal state. High emotion shortens the gap between stimulus and response, sharpens defensiveness, and makes it harder to choose your words rather than emit them. Self-regulation is the act of creating a pause — breathing, scanning your body, labeling the emotion — so that you respond from a steadier place.

This is distinct from staying quiet or playing it cool. It's an active internal process: noticing what's happening in your body, naming the state, and choosing how to re-enter the conversation. Regulation isn't suppression — it's the difference between reacting and deciding.

When to apply it

Under direct attack or criticism When you feel your heart rate spike mid-conversation Before a high-stakes conversation When you notice you're about to match someone's hostile energy

Key behaviors

  • Slow your breath before responding — try a shorter inhale and a longer exhale
  • Scan your body for tension before speaking; notice where you're holding it
  • Label the emotion internally before choosing words — naming it reduces its pull
  • Slow your cadence deliberately; rushing often signals dysregulation before your words do
  • Use the pause as an active tool, not dead air

What undermines it

  • Treating silence as weakness or loss of ground
  • Matching escalating energy automatically
  • Speaking before you've settled because you fear looking slow
  • Believing that fast responses signal confidence
