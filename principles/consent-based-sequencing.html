Principle 07 · Consent-based Sequencing

Align before advancing.

What it is

Consent-based sequencing is the practice of checking for alignment at each meaningful step in a conversation before moving to the next. Rather than presenting an argument or proposal as a complete package, you pause to verify that the other person is with you — that they share your read of the problem, agree with your framing, or are ready to hear what comes next.

Alignment checks surface misalignment early — before you've built a case on a faulty premise. They also give the other person room to redirect, which makes the conversation more accurate, not just more agreeable.

This is distinct from being organised or thorough. It's about treating a conversation as a series of gates rather than a monologue.

When to apply it

Presenting a proposal or recommendation Working through a complex problem together Pitching an idea to a skeptical audience Delivering difficult or unwelcome feedback Before offering a diagnosis or interpretation

Key behaviors

  • Before presenting your solution, check that the other person agrees on the problem: "Does that match what you're seeing?"
  • Ask permission before shifting into advice mode: "Can I share what I'm noticing?"
  • After laying out context, pause before concluding: "Does that framing make sense to you?"
  • When you notice hesitation or silence, name what you're observing before continuing: "You've gone quiet — what's your read on this?"
  • Treat silence or vague agreement as a reason to check, not a green light to proceed
  • Break complex proposals into steps and confirm each before building on it

What undermines it

  • Treating rhetorical questions as genuine checks — "Does that make sense?" delivered without actually waiting for an answer
  • Advancing past a soft objection without addressing it
  • Conflating silence with agreement
  • Asking for alignment only at the end, after the full argument has been presented — by then it's too late to catch early misalignment
  • Using alignment checks to engineer "yes" responses toward a predetermined conclusion rather than genuinely testing understanding
