Principle 14

State your limits; don't argue for them

Boundary-Setting

What it is

A boundary isn't a negotiating position — it's a statement of what you will and won't do. When you justify your limits, you invite debate. When you explain at length why something isn't okay, you signal that your limit is provisional — open to a good enough counter-argument.

The limit stands because you've decided it does, not because you've successfully defended it. The approach: state the limit clearly, calmly, and without defence. "I won't do that." Not "I can't, because…" Not "I wish I could, but…" You can acknowledge the other person's underlying need without treating your limit as the thing under negotiation.

When to apply it

When asked to do something outside your values or capacity When a previous "no" is being relitigated When someone pushes back on a limit you've already stated When you find yourself over-explaining a decision

Key behaviors

  • Use "I won't" not "I can't" — the distinction matters, to you as much as to them
  • State the limit once, clearly and calmly
  • Redirect rather than re-justify: "I've already addressed that"
  • Don't justify, argue, defend, or explain your limits beyond one clear statement
  • Keep your voice level and your pace deliberate — urgency signals the limit is still in play

What undermines it

  • Over-explaining as a form of appeasement
  • Treating pushback as a reason to reconsider
  • Using "I can't" when you mean "I won't"
  • Apologising for having limits
