Principle 13

Surface the hidden conversation, starting with your own

Emotional Awareness

What it is

Most conversations have two layers: the surface topic and the hidden conversation underneath — the fear, the frustration, the feeling of being dismissed. When you only engage the surface, the hidden conversation keeps driving. A reaction that seems disproportionate to its trigger usually isn't; something else is at work.

The first place to look is yourself. Before you can notice what's driving someone else's response, you need to know what's driving yours. Surfacing the hidden conversation starts internally — checking your own emotional state, naming it, and separating it from the content at hand. From that position you can engage the other person's subtext without projecting your own onto it.

When to apply it

When a reaction seems disproportionate to the trigger When someone shuts down or escalates unexpectedly When a conversation keeps circling back to the same point When your own reaction feels bigger than the situation warrants

Key behaviors

  • Check your own hidden conversation first: "What's actually going on for me right now?"
  • Pause before assuming the surface topic is the whole conversation
  • Acknowledge the feeling before engaging the content: "It sounds like this has been frustrating for a while"
  • Where there's enough trust, name what you notice — and turn it into an invitation: "I'm sensing there's more here than the deadline. Am I reading that right?"
  • Ask rather than tell: "What's going on for you?" opens more than "You seem upset about X"

What undermines it

  • Taking surface complaints at pure face value when the emotional signal is louder
  • Labelling emotion as irrational rather than informative
  • Moving to solutions before the feeling has been acknowledged
  • Naming someone else's subtext before you've checked your own
