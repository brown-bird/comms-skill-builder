Principle 02 · Clarity & Brevity

Say only what needs to be said.

What it is

Clarity means expressing your meaning precisely enough that the other person doesn't have to interpret or guess. Brevity means you don't force them to extract the signal from noise. Together, they respect the other person's time and cognitive load.

When to apply it

Making a request or ask Written messages and emails Status updates and reports Explaining a decision Setting expectations Delegating work

Key behaviors

  • Lead with the point, then support it — don't bury the ask at the end
  • Name the specific action you need, by when, and why it matters
  • Prefer concrete language over abstract: "by Thursday at noon" not "soon"
  • Cut anything that doesn't add meaning — filler, hedging, excessive context
  • Before sending, re-read as if you're the recipient: is the ask clear, and does anything here not need to be there?
  • Separate multiple asks into distinct items rather than layering them

What undermines it

  • Hinting instead of asking — hoping the other person will infer what you want
  • Over-qualifying to soften the message until the point is lost
  • Front-loading so much context that the actual ask gets lost
  • Using jargon or acronyms with people who don't share your reference frame

  • A peer is taking credit for your work
  • Negotiating a flexible working arrangement
  • Saying no to working on the weekend
  • Giving critical feedback to a peer
