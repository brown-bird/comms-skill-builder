Principle 05

Principled Negotiation

Focus on interests, not positions. The stated demand is rarely the whole story — and agreements built on underlying interests tend to hold.

What it is

Principled negotiation (drawn from the work of Fisher and Ury) is the practice of separating people from the problem, focusing on shared interests rather than stated positions, generating options before committing, and grounding decisions in objective criteria rather than power or pressure.

It treats negotiation as a collaborative problem to solve rather than a contest to win — which usually produces better outcomes for both sides.

When to apply it

Salary or compensation discussions Project scope and priority setting Dividing responsibilities Resource allocation Family decisions with competing preferences Vendor or partner agreements

Key behaviors

  • Ask "why" behind stated positions: "Help me understand what's important to you about that"
  • Name your own interests, not just your position: "What I'm trying to achieve is…"
  • Brainstorm multiple options before evaluating any of them
  • Anchor proposals in objective criteria (market data, precedent, shared standards)
  • Know your BATNA (best alternative to negotiated agreement) before entering
  • Look for trades that are low-cost to you and high-value to them, and vice versa

What undermines it

  • Treating the negotiation as zero-sum before exploring alternatives
  • Anchoring too hard on your opening position
  • Mixing relationship repair with substantive negotiation
  • Agreeing to terms you can't actually commit to, just to close the conversation
