Principle 12

Own your words — drop the hedge

Assertive Communication

What it is

Hedging language signals doubt — not just to others, but to yourself. "I just think maybe…" "I'm not sure but…" "You probably already know this…" These phrases soften your position before it's even heard, and they invite the other person to discount what comes next.

Owning your words means speaking directly, using "I" statements, and trusting that your message doesn't need to be pre-apologised. Directness isn't coldness — it's respect for both your message and your listener. You can be uncertain and still speak without hedging; the difference is between "I'm not sure this is right" and "I just thought maybe it might possibly be worth considering..."

When to apply it

When making a request When stating a need or a limit When giving feedback When sharing a perspective in a group setting

Key behaviors

  • Start sentences with "I" not "I just"
  • State needs as needs: "I need..." not "Would it be possible…?"
  • Drop filler apologies before direct statements — say the thing, then stop
  • State uncertainty cleanly: "I'm not sure" rather than a chain of qualifiers
  • Say "I'm confident" when you are — it signals ownership, not arrogance

What undermines it

  • Apologising before asserting
  • Using passive voice to avoid ownership
  • Framing requirements as suggestions
  • Softening every direct statement with a follow-up qualifier
