Principle 11

Speak to connect, not to win

Conversational Intent

What it is

Conversations aren't contests. When you enter one trying to win — to be right, to have the last word, to land the decisive point — you start optimising for score rather than understanding. The two goals pull in opposite directions. The more you press to win, the less the other person can hear you.

This is distinct from acknowledging before you advance, which addresses how to respond when someone is in distress. This principle is about the intent you carry into a disagreement — staying oriented toward contact and exchange rather than conquest, even when you're confident you're right.

It doesn't mean avoiding disagreement or letting bad ideas slide. It means holding your intent clearly: you're here to understand and be understood, and you measure success by whether the relationship can sustain a next conversation.

When to apply it

In disagreements with people you'll need to work with again When you feel the urge to prove a point When the relationship is more valuable than the outcome When you notice you're building a case rather than having a conversation

Key behaviors

  • Ask yourself before speaking: "Am I trying to understand, or trying to prevail?"
  • Prioritise understanding over agreement — you can disagree and still connect
  • Resist the urge to score points even when you could
  • Notice when you've shifted from conversation to argument, and name it: "I think we're going in circles — can we back up?"
  • Measure success by the quality of the next interaction, not the outcome of this one

What undermines it

  • Treating every disagreement as a referendum on who's smarter
  • Needing the last word
  • Conflating being right with being effective
