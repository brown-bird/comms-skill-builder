Professional Communication Skills

1. Active Listening in Meetings

Give full attention without planning your response while others speak. Show engagement through body language and brief acknowledgments.

Try this: "Just to make sure I understand correctly, you're saying [summarize their point]. Is that right?"

2. Clear, Direct Requests

Replace vague hints with specific asks. State what you need, by when, and why it matters.

Instead of: "It would be nice if someone could look at this..."
Say: "Sarah, could you review section 3 by Thursday? I need your input before the client call."

3. Constructive Feedback

Use the "observation + impact + request" framework. Focus on specific behaviors, not character.

Framework: "When [specific behavior], it [impact]. Could you [specific request]?"
Example: "When reports come in late, it delays our client updates. Could you send them by 3pm on Fridays?"

4. Managing Difficult Conversations

Prepare key points, choose the right time/place, and stay calm. Focus on solutions, not blame.

Opening: "I'd like to discuss [topic]. Is now a good time, or should we schedule 15 minutes later today?"

⚡ Quick Professional Tips

  • Pause 2 seconds before responding in tense conversations
  • Use "and" instead of "but" to build on ideas, not dismiss them
  • Match your communication style to your audience (formal/casual)
  • Follow up important verbal conversations with brief email summaries
  • Ask "What questions do you have?" instead of "Any questions?"

Family Communication Skills

1. Emotional Validation

Acknowledge feelings before problem-solving. People need to feel heard before they can hear you.

Try this: "That sounds really frustrating. I can see why you'd feel that way." (Then pause and listen more)

2. "I" Statements vs "You" Statements

Express your feelings and needs without blaming. This reduces defensiveness.

Instead of: "You never help around the house!"
Say: "I feel overwhelmed when I'm handling all the chores. I need more support."

3. Timing and Tone

Bring up sensitive topics when both parties are calm, not during conflict or when someone's stressed.

Opening: "I'd like to talk about something important. Is this a good time, or would after dinner work better?"

4. Repair After Conflict

Take responsibility for your part, even if it's just 5%. Apologize specifically, not generally.

Instead of: "Sorry if I upset you."
Say: "I'm sorry I raised my voice. That wasn't fair to you."

5. Regular Check-ins

Don't wait for problems. Create routine moments to connect and discuss how things are going.

Try this: Weekly 20-minute conversations about: What went well? What was hard? What do we need from each other?

⚡ Quick Family Tips

  • Put phones away during important conversations
  • Use the "24-hour rule" - wait before responding to hurtful messages
  • Ask permission before giving advice: "Do you want my thoughts or just to vent?"
  • Notice and mention what family members do well, not just problems
  • Set boundaries with love: "I care about you AND I need..."
